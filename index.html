Meet Monica Hewryk.

Monica is a highly accomplished ceramic artist, specializing in the creation of exquisite ceramic pieces.She earned her Bachelor of Arts degree with a concentration in ceramics from Central Connecticut State University in 2014, followed by a Master of Fine Arts degree in ceramics from Edinboro University of Pennsylvania in 2021. With a strong foundation in academia, she currently serves as a University Assistant at Central Connecticut State University. Additionally, she worked as an intern at Wesleyan Potters, where she further developed her craft under the guidance of experienced artisans and as a Resident Potter at the Guilford Art Center.

Monica's artistic journey has been punctuated by numerous successes, showcasing her talent and dedication. She has been actively participating in juried exhibitions and group shows since 2013, consistently impressing audiences with her exceptional craftsmanship and artistic vision. Driven by a passion for her craft, Monica maintains a private studio at home, where she continues to create captivating ceramic works.

Artist Statement

Artist Statement

The reality that time is fleeting is always on my mind. This body of ceramic sculptures focuses on expressing how time influences my emotions. A repeated endeavor that I often experience is a feeling of perpetually racing against time. Keeping excessively busy has always been a coping mechanism of mine for avoiding emotional struggles. Iâ€™m always trying to find balance between meeting deadlines and making time for loved ones, but time is constantly ticking.

I find joy in creating semi-realistic objects out of clay to help tell my personal narrative. My intent is to accentuate the cause and effect of unwanted growth and decay by creating grotesque textured surfaces on my ceramic pieces. Their presence is emphasized through the use of contrasting colors against their decaying surfaces.

The abrupt and devastating loss of both my mother and brother has led me to explore the expression of the negative effects that trauma has on my emotional well-being. The human condition exists as an imperfect entity because the presence of death is inevitable. I intentionally destroy parts of the work to emphasize imperfection, inevitability and disease while leaving enough of the piece to portray memory, or the visual evidence of what used to be.
